01 - The Throughput Trap

The industry is optimizing for the wrong variable.

Every frontier AI lab optimizes for the same objective: serve the maximum number of users with the minimum compute per response. This is the correct optimization if you are building a consumer product at global scale, but not necessarily so if you merely need the smartest possible answer.

During inference, models use only a fraction of the parallel compute available on modern GPUs. The rest is reserved for concurrency. Voidfall redirects that unused parallel capacity directly into generating a better response. You get to choose the tradeoff: fewer concurrent queries, but each one is measurably more intelligent.

This is not prompt engineering, chain-of-thought, or any other technique applied after the model generates its answer. Voidfall improves the quality of each individual token prediction. Every other technique still works on top, merely starting from a higher baseline.

02 - The Benchmark

Why you should believe these numbers.

ML benchmarks have a credibility problem. Data leakage — where evaluation questions overlap with pre-training data, even unintentionally — inflates scores in ways that are hard to detect and easy to ignore. We needed an evaluation where memorization is provably impossible.

Our benchmark uses Base-9 arithmetic with custom-defined operators that exist nowhere in any pre-training corpus. The model must learn novel mathematical rules from scratch and apply genuine multi-step reasoning. There is no shortcut.

The operator set

Four custom operators, each with a specific definition the model has never encountered, alongside standard arithmetic, all computed in Base-9. A fifth prefix also reverses operand order.

Problems are nested up to five levels deep, mixing custom and standard operators, requiring the model to hold intermediate results in working memory while applying unfamiliar rules. The benchmark covers five distinct reasoning tasks:

Deep Calculation
Calculate in Base-9:
(((13 # 8) $ 5) !@ (7 & 12))
Algebraic Inversion
Solve for x:
((x @ 5) # 3) = 42₉
Word Problem
A shield generator outputs 13₉ GJ. An auxiliary core adds 7₉ via the '@' conduit. What is the total capacity?
Comparison
Compare in Base-9:
(4 @ 8) # 3  vs  12 $ (6 & 2)

The model either intelligently reasons through these novel definitions, or it fails. No partial credit.

The experimental design

We take models from the same model family and finetune them on token-for-token identical training data with the same 9 million tokens, the same examples, the same order. We tested both a 3B and a 7B variant. When comparing the Voidfall method against the standard approach on the 3B model, the only variable is the method.

05 - Background

Where the insight came from.

Before Voidfall, I worked as an AI intern at NASA Ames Research Center, building language models for Space Medicine. The problem was concrete: when an astronaut on Mars faces a medical emergency outside their training, Earth could be twenty minutes away at the speed of light. The model needed to run on spacecraft-grade hardware, reason well, and operate with no cloud and no API. My specialization was quantization — making models as small as possible while preserving reasoning quality.

That experience made something visible. The gap between what a model could reason and what throughput-optimized inference allows it to reason is significant — and recoverable. Every major lab optimizes away from this gap because their economics demand concurrency. But for anyone who values intelligence over throughput, that gap is an untapped resource.

I spent the past year building a method to recover it. It works, it's measurable, and I haven't found a ceiling.
